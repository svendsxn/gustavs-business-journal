## Investment Casting (Lost Wax Casting) for Stainless Steel

Watched a video on how stainless steel objects are cast using the investment casting (lost wax) process.

**Video:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=TVsJlWEzZY8

**Key steps in the process:**
1. Wax pattern injection from an aluminum mold/die
2. Ceramic shell building (repeated dipping in slurry + stucco)
3. Dewaxing in an autoclave (melts the wax out)
4. Firing the ceramic shell
5. Pouring molten stainless steel
6. Shell knockout, cut-off, and grinding
7. Heat treatment and pickling

**Why this matters:** This is how valve bodies and other complex stainless steel components are manufactured. Understanding the casting process helps evaluate supplier quality, lead times, and cost drivers.

## Elon Book Notes: Knowledge Topics / Gold Nuggets

Rewrote a long Elon-focused discussion into compact operating principles for building companies, products, and teams.

### 1. Build What Must Exist

Do not start a company to become an entrepreneur.

Start with: What useful thing should exist that does not exist yet?

The best opportunities are often the problems nobody else is willing to touch.

Mission-first work can look riskier, but it often creates more unique value.

### 2. Product Builders Over Dealmakers

We do not need more people optimizing deals all day.

We need more people building products, companies, and infrastructure.

Societies advance when more talent goes into making things, not just moving money.

### 3. Mission Beats Money

Elon's decision rule is not: What has the best risk-adjusted return?

It is: What problem matters enough that it is worth attempting even if I fail?

A strong mission creates unusual courage, endurance, and clarity.

### 4. The Constraint Is Great Engineers

Capital is often not the real bottleneck.

The bottleneck is exceptional engineering talent.

Great companies are built by finding, trusting, and stretching unusually capable people.

### 5. Hire for Proof of Real Work

Look for people who solved hard, specific problems.

Ask detailed questions about what they personally did.

Real operators can explain the messy details.

People who never failed or cannot explain tradeoffs probably were not carrying the load.

### 6. Give Young Talent Real Responsibility

High-potential, unproven people can outperform credentialed veterans.

Especially in frontier work, bad habits from incumbents can be more costly than inexperience.

Find competence early, then increase responsibility fast.

### 7. Excellence Is the Capacity to Take Pain

Hard work is not the differentiator by itself.

The differentiator is the willingness to absorb pain, stress, pressure, and repeated setbacks without quitting.

Caring deeply about the mission makes pain tolerable.

### 8. Burn the Boats

Optionality is often overrated in serious company-building.

A backup plan can dilute intensity.

Many breakthroughs come only when retreat is psychologically removed.

### 9. Work on the Future, Not on Safe Commodities

Commodity businesses may be safer.

But unique, high-impact problems can create much greater value.

Adding new capability to humanity is a stronger strategic position than copying a proven model.

### 10. Engineering Is Modern Magic

If you build something that previously could not exist, that is a kind of magic.

Engineering is one of the deepest levers for civilizational progress.

Technological progress is not automatic. Humans must build it.

### 11. Know the Business From A to Z

Founders need deep firsthand understanding of the product, process, materials, and constraints.

Go to the line. Go to the machines. Go to the failure point.

The closer you are to reality, the better your decisions.

### 12. First Principles Over Industry Tradition

Do not ask: How is this usually done?

Ask: What do physics, materials, cost, and constraints actually require?

Industry "best practice" is often just accumulated habit.

### 13. The Algorithm

1. Question requirements.
2. Delete.
3. Simplify and optimize.
4. Accelerate.
5. Automate.

Most people do this backward.

They automate or optimize before deciding whether the thing should exist at all.

### 14. Make Requirements Less Dumb

Many requirements are inherited, outdated, political, or misunderstood.

Every requirement should have a named owner.

If nobody can defend a requirement clearly, it should probably be challenged or removed.

### 15. Delete Before You Improve

The best part is no part.

The best process is no process.

If you are not adding some things back after deletion, you probably did not delete enough.

### 16. Simplicity Creates Both Reliability and Low Cost

More parts mean more cost, more assembly, more failure points, more tolerance stack-up, more delays.

Simplicity is not aesthetic minimalism only.

It is operational leverage.

### 17. Don't Optimize What Shouldn't Exist

A common smart-person mistake: optimizing a bad assumption.

A faster bad process is still bad.

If you are digging your grave, do not dig faster.

### 18. Automate Last

Automation is expensive and rigid.

If you automate before cleaning up design and process, you lock in waste.

Manual or semi-manual work often reveals what should be deleted first.

### 19. Small Failures Are Good

Design organizations to fail cheaply, quickly, and repeatedly.

Failure is useful when it reveals truth without being catastrophic.

The goal is not avoiding mistakes.

The goal is learning faster than everyone else.

### 20. Deadlines Should Be Tight Enough to Miss Sometimes

If you hit every deadline, the deadlines are probably too soft.

Aggressive timelines create pressure, urgency, and faster iteration.

Missed deadlines are acceptable if they move the system faster overall.

### 21. Reality Is the Final Judge

Physics does not care about opinions, status, feelings, or ideology.

The product either works or it does not.

Reality-based iteration is the core learning loop in hard tech and serious company building.

### 22. Truth-Seeking Beats Wishful Thinking

Belief matters, but belief cannot override reality.

Strong builders test assumptions early and often.

Fast contact with reality is a competitive advantage.

### 23. Tip-of-the-Spear Focus

Always identify the main bottleneck.

Attack the biggest constraint first.

Do not distribute effort evenly across many secondary issues.

### 24. Solve the Bottleneck of the Bottleneck

Good operators identify the bottleneck.

Great operators keep drilling until they find the real bottleneck beneath it.

The limiting factor is often more specific than it first appears.

### 25. Speed Is a Strategic Weapon

Speed is both offense and defense.

A factory moving twice as fast is effectively two factories.

A company that learns faster compounds faster.

### 26. Time Is the Only True Currency

Money can be replaced.

Time cannot.

It is acceptable to waste equipment or cash in some cases.

It is not acceptable to waste critical time.

### 27. Avoid Serialized Dependencies

Do as much as possible in parallel.

Sequential work extends timelines dramatically.

Parallel work raises risk, but compresses time.

### 28. Vertical Integration Increases Speed

If you rely on the legacy supply chain, you inherit its speed, cost structure, and technological limits.

Vertical integration gives control over pace, quality, cost, and innovation.

This is especially important when aiming for order-of-magnitude improvement.

### 29. The Organization Should Mirror the Product

Poor organizational structure shows up in the product.

Silos create duplicate work, extra parts, extra interfaces, and worse decisions.

Designers, engineers, and manufacturing teams need tight feedback loops.

### 30. Lead From the Front

In hard periods, leaders need visible sacrifice.

Presence matters.

People work harder when they see leadership sharing the same discomfort and urgency.

### 31. Repetition Installs Operating Principles

Great builders do not rely on one-off inspiration.

They repeat a few core principles until the organization internalizes them.

Repetition is not redundancy. It is how culture becomes action.

### 32. One Metric, One Objective

Teams need one dominant metric per mission.

Clarity beats complexity.

A single visible target aligns thousands of decisions.

### 33. Direct Accountability

Vague ownership creates weak execution.

Every decision, requirement, and metric should have a directly responsible person.

Accountability accelerates clarity.

### 34. High-Leverage Thinking Matters

In high-scale companies, one high-quality decision can create enormous value.

Senior time should go to the most leveraged bottleneck, not to ceremony.

Great founders constantly ask: What is the most useful thing I can do right now?

### 35. A Founder's Job Is to Beat Back Bureaucracy

Growth naturally creates meetings, layers, and process.

The founder must repeatedly remove what slows the company down.

Simplicity is not a one-time act. It is continuous maintenance.

### 36. Make Stuff

Economies are not magic.

If people do not build, produce, manufacture, and deliver, there is no "stuff."

Real progress depends on people who make useful things and useful services.

### 37. Useful Output Should Be Applauded

Building and shipping real products is extremely hard.

Companies that create useful products raise living standards.

That kind of value creation deserves respect.

### 38. Encourage More Builders

A worthy ambition is not to create more spectators.

It is to create more builders.

The ideal outcome is not more people copying Elon.

It is more people choosing difficult, meaningful problems and dedicating themselves to solving them.
